Goal: The Gromov-Witten Invariants
==================================

::: {.warnings .proofenv}
These are extremely rough and imprecise notes taken for a reading seminar. They're very likely full of mistakes and misunderstandings, so please use at your own risk.
:::

::: {.remark .proofenv}
Our long-term goal: define the **Gromov-Witten** invariants of (say) a symplectic 4-manifold, i.e.Â a complex symplectic surface. These will be notated something like ${\operatorname{GW}}(M, A, g, n)$, where

-   $(M, \omega, J)$ is a symplectic manifold with an almost-complex structure $J$,
-   $A\in H_2(M; {\mathbb{Z}})$ is a fixed homology class,
-   $g,n$ denote the genus and number of marked points of a complex curve (Riemann surface) $\Sigma_{g, n}$ we're mapping into $M$.

The point of these invariants is to take some kind of enumerative count of rigid complex curves (*$J{\hbox{-}}$holomorphic curves*) embedded into $M$, up to some notion of isomorphism so that we can make this a finite count. The $J{\hbox{-}}$holomorphic condition is essentially that these curves are in the kernel of some differential operator that generalizes the usual Cauchy-Riemann equations from 1-dimensional complex analysis. The actual count will be the number of curves that are homologically equivalent to the fixed class $A$.

The rough program is to form some moduli space of curves, "rigidify" it enough to get it to be a finite-dimensional smooth manifold, and try to relate the curve count to the dimension of the moduli space. We'll then relate that dimension to the analytic *index* of a Cauchy-Riemann operator $D$, and use a mix of analysis, topology, and algebraic geometry to compute this index (either by computing kernels/cokernels directly or relating them to other invariants like integrals, Chern classes, etc).
:::

> Note: I'll be writing $\operatorname{Sym}^n(X)$ everywhere for convenience, but I don't know if this is true on the nose -- in the older literature these maps are all defined to land in $M^{\times n}$ instead.

::: {.remark .proofenv}
Fix $M, A, J, g, n$. We'll define $\mkern 1.5mu\overline{\mkern-1.5mu{\mathcal{M}}\mkern-1.5mu}\mkern 1.5mu_{g, n}(A, J)$ to be the moduli space of embedded $J{\hbox{-}}$holomorphic curves $\Sigma \xhookrightarrow{f} M$ with $[\Sigma] = [A]$ in $H_2(M; {\mathbb{Z}})$ where $g(\Sigma) = g$ and ${\left[ {{ {x}_1, {x}_2, \cdots, {x}_{n}}} \right]} \in \operatorname{Sym}^n \Sigma$ is a collection of $n$ (distinct?) marked points.
:::

::: {.remark .proofenv}
There is a well-studied moduli space $\mkern 1.5mu\overline{\mkern-1.5mu{\mathcal{M}}\mkern-1.5mu}\mkern 1.5mu_{g, n}$, *the* moduli space of curves, which shows up in a thousand different guises with a thousand different names. It is a very good space that a lot of people like. We'll define it here to be the moduli space of **stable** embedded curves $\Sigma\hookrightarrow M$ with $g(\Sigma) = g$ where again $\Sigma$ carries $n$ marked points. The stability condition is something we'll cover later.

In full generality, $\mkern 1.5mu\overline{\mkern-1.5mu{ \mathcal{M}_{g, n} }\mkern-1.5mu}\mkern 1.5mu$ is a smooth Deligne-Mumford stack -- i.e.Â a somewhat complicated algebro-geometric object. One slogan that may be helpful: the category of schemes isn't closed under quotients, but schemes embed into stacks and stacks are closed under taking quotients. So you might think of a stack as the quotient of a scheme by a non-free group action, where you might even take an algebraic group instead of just a Lie group or something. A Deligne-Mumford stack will just be a stack that is stratified by quotient stacks.
:::

::: {.remark .proofenv}
It may be helpful to think of $\mkern 1.5mu\overline{\mkern-1.5mu{\mathcal{M}}\mkern-1.5mu}\mkern 1.5mu_{g, n}$ as a complex *orbifold*. As a first approximation, an *orbifold* is just a manifold with some exceptional collection of singular "orbifold points", which are the fixed points of some group action. Here's the cartoon I usually have in mind:

![[figures/2021-06-26_17-57-58.png]]

For genus $g = 0$, $\mkern 1.5mu\overline{\mkern-1.5mu{\mathcal{M}}\mkern-1.5mu}\mkern 1.5mu_{0, n}$ will be a smooth compact complex manifold of finite dimension, so we can run arguments from smooth/differential topology if we just map in $J{\hbox{-}}$holomorphic spheres.
:::

::: {.definition .proofenv title="Gromov-Witten invariants, preliminary definition"}
There are two natural maps floating around:

`<p style="text-align:center;"> <img class="tikzcd" src="figures/c4de8212550fb084cec674b1b08625a1e9e1f2d6.svg"></p>`{=html}

> [Link to Diagram](https://q.uiver.app/?q=WzAsMyxbMCwwLCJcXGJhcntcXG1jbX1fe2csIG59KEEsIEopIl0sWzAsMiwiXFxiYXJ7XFxtY219X3tnLCBufSJdLFsyLDIsIlxcU3ltXm4oWCkiXSxbMCwxLCJcXHRleHR7Rm9yZ2V0IH1BLCBKIiwyXSxbMCwyLCJcXGV2XzEsIFxcZXZfMiwgXFxjZG90cywgXFxldl9uIiwwLHsibGV2ZWwiOjN9XV0=)

The evaluation maps $\operatorname{ev}_j$ are coming from the fact that every point in the moduli space upstairs carries the data of an embedding $f: \Sigma_{g, n} \hookrightarrow M$, and if $x_i$ is a marked point we can just push it forward and look at $f(x_i) \subseteq M$. So morally speaking, the GW invariants will be defined as `
<span class="math display">
\begin{align*}
{\operatorname{GW}}(M, A, g, n):
H^*(M; {\mathbb{Q}})^{\otimes_{\mathbb{Q}}n} \otimes_{\mathbb{Q}}H^*({ \mkern 1.5mu\overline{\mkern-1.5mu\mathcal{M}\mkern-1.5mu}\mkern 1.5mu_{g, n} }; {\mathbb{Q}}) &\to {\mathbb{Q}}\\
({ {\alpha}_1, {\alpha}_2, \cdots, {\alpha}_{n}}) \otimes\beta &\mapsto \int_{{ \mkern 1.5mu\overline{\mkern-1.5mu\mathcal{M}\mkern-1.5mu}\mkern 1.5mu_{g, n} }(A, J)} \prod_{j=1}^n \operatorname{ev}_j^*(\alpha_j) \smile\pi^*(\beta^\mathrm{PD})
,\end{align*}
<span>`{=html} where here the product denotes the $n{\hbox{-}}$fold cup product in (say, singular) cohomology.
:::

::: {.remark .proofenv}
The output will be the number of embedded $J{\hbox{-}}$holomorphic curves $\Sigma$

-   Where $g(\Sigma) = g$
-   With $n$ marked points $x_i$
-   Where $x_i$ intersects a cycle $X_i \subseteq H_2(M; {\mathbb{Q}})$
-   Where $X_i$ is dual to $\alpha_i$
-   Where $[\pi(\Sigma)] = [\beta] \in H_2({ \mkern 1.5mu\overline{\mkern-1.5mu\mathcal{M}\mkern-1.5mu}\mkern 1.5mu_{g, n} })$.

So roughly the number of curves representing the homology class $\beta$, and we get it by "integrating over the moduli space" in the sense of capping against a fundamental class.
:::

::: {.warnings .proofenv}
This description is partially a cartoon! It will work in certain special cases, but ${ \mkern 1.5mu\overline{\mkern-1.5mu\mathcal{M}\mkern-1.5mu}\mkern 1.5mu_{g, n} }$ doesn't have an honest fundamental class in general to integrate against. Some hard work of e.g.Â Fantechi constructs a "virtual fundamental class" that (I think) more faithfully captures this idea.
:::

::: {.example .proofenv title="Uses"}
GW invariants can be used to get at classical enumerative problems. For example, we can compute the number of lines in ${\mathbb{P}}^3({\mathbb{C}})$ intersecting 4 generic lines as `
<span class="math display">
\begin{align*}
{\operatorname{GW}}_{L, 4}^{{\mathbb{P}}^3}(c^2, c^2, c^2, c^2) = 2
.\end{align*}
<span>`{=html}
:::

::: {.remark .proofenv}
The GW invariants will only depend on the **deformation type** of $(M, \omega)$. In particular, we'll be able to take 1-parameter families of symplectic manifolds constructed by cooking up paths `
<span class="math display">
\begin{align*}
\gamma: I&\to \Omega^2(M) \\
t &\mapsto \omega_t
,\end{align*}
<span>`{=html} all of which will have the same GW invariants, provided we start with *semipositive* symplectic manifolds and choose these paths carefully. Morally, this is moving the manifolds $(M, \omega)$ around in the moduli space, just in a controlled way (along semipositive families) as opposed to just wiggling in an ${\varepsilon}$ ball in ${ \mkern 1.5mu\overline{\mkern-1.5mu\mathcal{M}\mkern-1.5mu}\mkern 1.5mu_{g, n} }(A, J)$.
:::

::: {.remark .proofenv}
A remarkable (and hard?) theorem is that in real dimension 4, the GW invariants only depend on the *diffeomorphism type* of the manifold, and can detect non-diffeomorphic smooth manifolds. They are also equal to the Seiberg-Witten invariants in this dimension. This is not a general phenomenon though -- there are counterexamples in dimension 6 where neither of these statements hold.
:::

::: {.remark .proofenv}
The later chapters of the book discuss some applications to other topics. I'll just relay the words here, in case anything is meaningful to you all, since I don't know much about them yet myself:

-   For $g=0$, GW is related to quantum cohomology and Frobenius manifolds. There is some theorem about proving the associativity in quantum cohomology.
-   There is *some* way to produce a TQFT in this setting as well, and lots of people like these.
-   Mirror symmetry is supposed to give a 2nd way to compute these invariants. I think the symplectic side covered here corresponds to the "A side", and conjecturally there is a "B side" mirror with the same GW invariants. The book is a little old now, so I don't know how conjectural this still is.
:::

Ch. 3: Moduli Spaces and Transversality
=======================================

::: {.remark .proofenv}
Our goal for this chapter: show that for a general $J$, the moduli space ${\mathcal{M}}^*(A, \Sigma, J)$ is a smooth complex manifold of finite dimension. The asterisk here corresponds to taking only *simple curves* -- this doesn't seem to be a necessary condition, but is meant to make transversality arguments simpler. Here's a rough outline of the sections:

-   3.1: Defines the moduli space of simple curves

-   3.2: Discusses Thom-Smale transversality, and shows that ${\mathcal{M}}^*$ is a smooth manifold when the (linearized) Cauchy-Riemann operator $D$ is surjective for all $J{\hbox{-}}$holomorphic curves.

-   3.3 (today): Discusses examples of **regularity** in dimension 4, along with some sufficient conditions to determine if your favorite almost complex structure $J$ is sufficiently regular. The usual proofs lean on things like the Sard-Smale theorem, but here we'll use some AG techniques like the Riemann-Roch theorem to check these conditions.

-   3.4: Discusses moduli spaces with pointwise constraints.
:::

::: {.remark .proofenv}
Some structures to recall from Han's talks:

-   $(M, \omega, J)$ will be a $2n{\hbox{-}}$dimensional symplectic manifold, with $\omega \in\Omega^2(M)$ a symplectic form, $J$ an $\omega{\hbox{-}}$tame almost-complex structure on $M$, so $J\in \mathop{\mathrm{End}}(TM)$ with $J^2 = -\operatorname{id}$.

-   $(\Sigma, j_\Sigma, \,dV)$ will be a Riemann surface with an almost-complex structure and $\,dV$ its volume form. Note that in dimension 2, all almost-complex structures are *integrable* in the sense that they come from an honest complex structure, so we'll always think of $j_\Sigma$ as an actual complex structure.

    -   The Cauchy-Riemann operator ${ \mkern 1.5mu\overline{\mkern-1.5mu{\partial}\mkern-1.5mu}\mkern 1.5mu}_J \approx {1\over 2}\qty{Jd - d j_\sigma}$, where I'm being **very** loose with this definition! Just recall that (exercise) the usual Cauchy-Riemann equations can be generalized by $dJ -Jd = 0$ where $J$ is the standard complex structure on ${\mathbb{C}}^n$, and here we just allow the two complex structures to vary in the domain/codomain.
    -   Also $\ker { \mkern 1.5mu\overline{\mkern-1.5mu{\partial}\mkern-1.5mu}\mkern 1.5mu}_J$ are precisely the $J{\hbox{-}}$holomorphic curves, so solutions to this generalized Cauchy-Riemann equation.

-   $u\in C^\infty(\Sigma, M)$ will be a smooth map representing a solution. Note that we wanted Sobolev completions to some $W^{k, p}$ in order to apply PDE theory to $u$. In particular, we'll want the linearized ${ \mkern 1.5mu\overline{\mkern-1.5mu{\partial}\mkern-1.5mu}\mkern 1.5mu}_J$ to be a Fredholm operator so that it has a well-defined index `
    <span class="math display">
    \begin{align*}
    \mathop{\mathrm{ind}}(D) \coloneqq\dim_{\mathbb{R}}\ker(D) - \dim_{\mathbb{R}}\operatorname{coker}(D)
    .\end{align*}
    <span>`{=html} These are supposed to be like "operators that are invertible up to finite-dimensional noise", and such operators (and their indices) are stable under small perturbations.

-   ${\mathcal{M}}^*(A, \Sigma, J)$ will be $u\in C^\infty(\Sigma, M) \cap\ker { \mkern 1.5mu\overline{\mkern-1.5mu{\partial}\mkern-1.5mu}\mkern 1.5mu}_J$ with $[u(\Sigma)] = A\in H_2(M; {\mathbb{Z}})$ and $u$ a **simple** curve.

    -   Simple curves are defined by the following condition: a curve $u:\Sigma\to M$ is *not simple* iff there exists a branched cover $\tilde\Sigma\to \Sigma$ of degree $d\geq 2$ and an embedding $\tilde u: \tilde \Sigma\to M$ making the following diagram commute:

3.3: Regularity
---------------

::: {.definition .proofenv title="Regular"}
In light of the previous discussion, we'll say that $J$ is **regular** for $A$ iff $D_u$ is surjective for all $u\in {\mathcal{M}}^*(A, \Sigma, J)$ where the $J$ is fixed. A point $p \coloneqq(u, J) \in {\mathcal{M}}^*$ will be a **regular point** iff $T_p {\mathcal{M}}^*  \xrightarrow[]{d\pi_p} { \mathrel{\mkern-16mu}\rightarrow }\,  T_{\pi(p)} {\mathcal{J}}$ is surjective, where now we let $J$ vary in ${\mathcal{J}}$.
:::

::: {.remark .proofenv}
Recall that ${\mathcal{J}}$ is the space of all almost-complex structures on $M$. A consequence of regularity is that any smooth one-parameter family $[0, 1]\to {\mathcal{J}}$ can be ${\varepsilon}{\hbox{-}}$lifted in the sense that there is a commutative diagram

`<p style="text-align:center;"> <img class="tikzcd" src="figures/d5c378bec9ee28d8134dba1836c39805df7149e3.svg"></p>`{=html}

Moreover if $(u, J)$ is regular, then the lifts $(u, J_t)$ along the path upstairs will still be regular nearby:

![[figures/2021-06-26_19-28-31.png]]

Overview of Main Theorems
-------------------------

::: {.remark .proofenv}
The two main theorems of this section describe sufficient conditions for regularity and how to produce a regular almost-complex structure.
:::

::: {.theorem .proofenv title="3.3.4"}
Let $g(\Sigma) = 0$ and $\dim_{\mathbb{R}}M = 4$, and consider the $J{\hbox{-}}$holomorphically embedded sphere $(\Sigma, j_\Sigma) \hookrightarrow(M^4, J)$. Letting $p\coloneqq\Sigma^2 \coloneqq\Sigma \cdot \Sigma$ be the self-intersection number of $\Sigma$, then $J$ is regular for $A \coloneqq[\Sigma]$ if and only if $p\geq -1$.
:::

::: {.theorem .proofenv title="3.3.5"}
Let $\tilde A \coloneqq[S^2 \times{\operatorname{pt}}]$ as a class in $H_2(\tilde M; {\mathbb{Z}})$ where $\tilde M \coloneqq S^2 \times M$. Then for all $J \in {\mathcal{J}}(M, \omega)$, the almost-complex structure $\tilde J \coloneqq i\times J$ is regular for $\tilde A$, where $i$ is the standard complex structure on $S^2$.
:::

::: {.remark .proofenv}
The following is a summary of the other lemmas in this chapter, which are useful on their own but also used to prove the above two theorems.

-   3.3.1: If $J$ is integrable and ${\mathbb{CP}}^1 \xrightarrow{u} M$, then $u^* TM = \oplus L_k$ decomposes as a sum of line bundles and $J$ is regular iff $c_1(L_k) \geq -1$ for all $k$, where $c_1$ denotes the Chern number.

-   3.3.2: If ${\mathcal{E}}\to {\mathbb{CP}}^1$ is *any* bundle, not just $u^* TM$, and there exists a decomposition ${\mathcal{E}}= \oplus L_k$ into line bundles, and if $D: \Omega^0({\mathbb{CP}}^1; {\mathcal{E}}) \to \Omega^{0, 1}({\mathbb{CP}}^1; {\mathcal{E}})$ is any ${\mathbb{R}}{\hbox{-}}$linear Cauchy-Riemann operator that preserves the decomposition in the sense that $D(L_k ) \subseteq L_k$ for all $k$, then $D_u$ is surjective iff $c_1(L_k) \geq -1$ for all $k$.

-   3.3.3: If $(M, \omega, J)$ is any 4-dimensional symplectic manifold and $J$ is any almost-complex structure (not necessarily integrable) and $u: {\mathbb{CP}}^1 \to M$ is an *immersed* $J{\hbox{-}}$holomorphic sphere, then $D_u$ is surjective if $c_1(u^* TM) \geq 1$.

The rest of the section involves examples and constructions.
:::

Lemma 3.3.1
-----------

::: {.lemma .proofenv title="3.3.1"}
If $u^* TM \cong \oplus_{k=1}^\ell L_k$ and $c_1(L_k) \geq 1$ for every $k$, then $D_u$ is surjective.
:::

::: {.remark .proofenv}
To prove this, we'll need an analytic version of Riemann-Roch:
:::

::: {.theorem .proofenv title="Riemann-Roch, Append C.1.10, Part 3"}
If ${\mathcal{E}}\to \Sigma$ is a holomorphic bundle and $F\leq {\mathcal{E}}$ is a sub-bundle, then $D_u$ is surjective iff `
<span class="math display">
\begin{align*}
\mu({\mathcal{E}}, F) + 2\chi(\Sigma) > 0
,\end{align*}
<span>`{=html} where $\mu({-}, {-})$ is a **relative Maslov index**. Moreover, taking $F = \emptyset$, if ${{\partial}}\Sigma = \emptyset$ then there is a formula `
<span class="math display">
\begin{align*}
\mu({\mathcal{E}}) \coloneqq\mu({\mathcal{E}}, \emptyset) = 2 {\left\langle {c_1({\mathcal{E}})},~{ [\Sigma] } \right\rangle}
.\end{align*}
<span>`{=html}
:::

Proof using Riemann-Roch
------------------------

::: {.proof .proofenv title="of Lemma, using Riemann-Roch"}
We'll first need that since $\Sigma$ is a sphere, we know its cohomology ring: `
<span class="math display">
\begin{align*}
H^*(S^2; {\mathbb{Z}}) \cong {\mathbb{Z}}[x]/ \left\langle{x^2}\right\rangle && \text{where } {\left\lvert {x} \right\rvert} = 2
,\end{align*}
<span>`{=html} which is only supported in degrees $d=0, 2$. So `
<span class="math display">
\begin{align*}
\chi(\Sigma) = 1 - 0 + 1 = 2
.\end{align*}
<span>`{=html}

> Note that you could also just check this cellularly: $S^2$ has a CW complex structure with one 0-cell and one 2-cell, and you can compute $\chi$ using just the ranks of cellular chain groups instead of homology.

Strategy: take the LHS appearing in the RR formula above, we'll try to show it's positive. `
<span class="math display">
\begin{align*}
\mu(u^* TM) + 2\chi(\Sigma) 
&= \mu(u^* TM) + 4 \\
&= \mu\qty{ \bigoplus_{k=1}^\ell L_k } + 4 \\
&= \sum_{k=1}^\ell \mu\qty{ L_k } + 4 \\
&= \sum_{k=1}^\ell 2c_1(L_k) + 4
,\end{align*}
<span>`{=html} and thus `
<span class="math display">
\begin{align*}
2\sum_{k=1}^\ell c_1(L_k) + 4 & > 0 \\
\iff \sum_{k=1}^\ell c_1(L_k) & > -2
.\end{align*}
<span>`{=html} Since the rank of $u^* TM$ is at least 2, there are at least 2 summands. So if every $c_1(L_k) > -1$, this inequality holds, and that is sufficient for $D_u$ to be surjective.
:::

Proof using AG/Chern Classes
----------------------------

::: {.proof .proofenv title="of lemma, using complex analytic arguments"}

We want to show $D_u$ is surjective, so it thus suffices to show $\operatorname{coker}{ \mkern 1.5mu\overline{\mkern-1.5mu{\partial}\mkern-1.5mu}\mkern 1.5mu}_J = 0$, where it's worth recalling a nice identification: `
<span class="math display">
\begin{align*}
\operatorname{coker}\qty{A \xrightarrow{f} B} \cong B/\operatorname{im}A
.\end{align*}
<span>`{=html} The actual definition is taking a pushout against the terminal object in your category:

The main thing to notice is that one is taking the homology with respect to ${ \mkern 1.5mu\overline{\mkern-1.5mu{\partial}\mkern-1.5mu}\mkern 1.5mu}$, so the bottom-right corner of the diamond just forms a 2-term chain complex and we get a kernel/cokernel pair.

So now it suffices to show that $H^{0, 1}_{ \mkern 1.5mu\overline{\mkern-1.5mu{\partial}\mkern-1.5mu}\mkern 1.5mu}(\Sigma; L) = 0$ (for $L\coloneqq L_k$ any of the bundle summands) whenever $c_1(L_k) \geq 1$ for all $L_k$. We'll need a definition:

::: {.definition .proofenv title="Canonical Bundle"}
Let $\Omega^1_\Sigma$ be the bundle of holomorphic 1-forms on $\Sigma$. Then the **canonical bundle** is defined as `
<span class="math display">
\begin{align*}
K_\Sigma \coloneqq\bigwedge\nolimits^{\dim \Sigma} \Omega^1_\Sigma = \Omega^2_{\Sigma}
,\end{align*}
<span>`{=html} which here coincides with the bundle of holomorphic 2-forms. It is sometimes written as $\omega_\Sigma$
:::

We can now apply **Kodaira-Serre duality**: `
<span class="math display">
\begin{align*}
H^{0, 1}_{{ \mkern 1.5mu\overline{\mkern-1.5mu{\partial}\mkern-1.5mu}\mkern 1.5mu}}(\Sigma; L) \xrightarrow{\sim} H_{{ \mkern 1.5mu\overline{\mkern-1.5mu{\partial}\mkern-1.5mu}\mkern 1.5mu}}^{1, 0}(\Sigma; L {}^{ \vee }\otimes K_\Sigma) {}^{ \vee }
,\end{align*}
<span>`{=html} where notably we've switched from antiholomorphic forms to holomorphic forms.

We'll also need **Kodaira vanishing**: If ${\mathcal{L}}\to \Sigma$ is a *positive* holomorphic line bundle, then `
<span class="math display">
\begin{align*}
H^{i}(\Sigma; {\mathcal{L}}\otimes K_\Sigma) = 0 && \forall\, i>0
.\end{align*}
<span>`{=html}

The book justifies the uses of this theorem here by saying $c_1({\mathcal{L}})$ can be interpreted as the self-intersection number of the zero section, and mumbles something about "positivity of intersections". I'm not really sure why this works!

A related fact (maybe a consequence?) is that ${\mathcal{L}}$ has nonzero holomorphic sections $\iff c_1({\mathcal{L}}) \geq 0$, so maybe positivity is related to positivity of Chern numbers.

Now setting ${\mathcal{L}}\coloneqq L {}^{ \vee }\otimes K_\Sigma$, playing around with the logic we find that if $c_1({\mathcal{L}}) < 0$ then ${\mathcal{L}}$ has *no* holomorphic sections, and for reasons unknown, this should imply that $H^{1, 0}_{{ \mkern 1.5mu\overline{\mkern-1.5mu{\partial}\mkern-1.5mu}\mkern 1.5mu}}(\Sigma; {\mathcal{L}}) {}^{ \vee }= 0$ and conclude the proof. In any case, let's just compute the Chern number:

`
<span class="math display">
\begin{align*}
c_1({\mathcal{L}})
&= c_1(L {}^{ \vee }) + c_1(K_\Sigma) \\
&= c_1(L {}^{ \vee }) - c_1(T \Sigma) \\
&= c_1(L {}^{ \vee }) - e_1(T S^1) && \text{since $c_1$ is a top class}\\
&= c_1(L {}^{ \vee }) + \qty{1 + (-1)^2} && \text{by a well-known formula for spheres} \\
&= c_1(L {}^{ \vee }) - 2 \\
&= - c_1(L) - 2
.\end{align*}
<span>`{=html} So now unwinding things, we have `
<span class="math display">
\begin{align*}
c_1({\mathcal{L}}) < 0 
\iff -c_1(L) - 2 &< 0 \\
\iff -c_1(L) &< 2 \\
\iff c_1(L) &> - 2 \\
\iff c_1(L) &\geq -1
.\end{align*}
<span>`{=html} which is exactly the condition appearing in the lemma. Running this same argument for every $L_k$ concludes the proof!
:::

::: {.remark .proofenv}
Note that we've used some special facts in that last calculation:

-   Using that $L {}^{ \vee }\cong L^{-1}$ for line bundles, $c_1(L {}^{ \vee }) = c_1(L^{-1}) = -c_1(L)$.
-   I don't think $c_1(A\otimes B) = c_1(A) + c_1(B)$ in general, this must be special for $B = K$ the canonical.
-   $c_1(K_X) = -c_1(TX)$ is a general fact, for complex manifolds at least. Apparently this is obvious from Chern-Weil theory, but you can also use `
    <span class="math display">
    \begin{align*}
    c_1(TX) = c_1(\operatorname{det}TX) \coloneqq c_1\qty{ \bigwedge\nolimits^{\text{top}} TX} \coloneqq c_1(K_X {}^{ \vee }) = c_1(K_X^{-1}) =  -c_1(K_X)
    .\end{align*}
    <span>`{=html}
-   The top Chern class is always the Euler class (almost by definition) when it makes sense.
:::
